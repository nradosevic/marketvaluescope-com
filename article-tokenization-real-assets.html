Tokenization of Real Assets: The Infrastructure Is Finally Ready

By Marcus Chen March 10, 2026 Fintech

The concept of tokenizing real-world assets—representing ownership of physical property, securities, or commodities as digital tokens on a blockchain—has been discussed in financial technology circles for nearly a decade. Early enthusiasm gave way to skepticism as pilot projects struggled with regulatory hurdles, custody challenges, and the fundamental question of what problem tokenization actually solved for traditional asset classes. Now, however, a convergence of regulatory clarity, institutional infrastructure, and market demand is accelerating adoption in ways that suggest the technology's moment may have finally arrived.

The numbers tell part of the story. Industry estimates suggest that tokenized real-world assets under management have grown from negligible amounts to over $10 billion, with projections suggesting exponential growth over the next five years. Major financial institutions including BlackRock, JPMorgan, and Goldman Sachs have launched or announced tokenization initiatives. Perhaps more significantly, central banks and securities regulators in key jurisdictions have moved from skeptical observation to active engagement, establishing frameworks that provide the legal certainty necessary for institutional participation.

Understanding why tokenization matters requires looking beyond the blockchain technology itself to the inefficiencies it addresses. Traditional securities settlement involves multiple intermediaries, each maintaining their own records and charging fees for reconciliation services. Settlement cycles, though improved from historical norms, still create counterparty risk and tie up capital. Cross-border transactions face additional friction from currency conversion, regulatory compliance, and time zone mismatches. Tokenization, properly implemented, can compress these processes dramatically: trades settling in minutes rather than days, ownership records maintained on shared infrastructure, and programmable compliance reducing manual verification requirements.

The asset classes attracting the most institutional attention reveal where tokenization offers the clearest advantages. Private credit, with its complex documentation and illiquid secondary markets, benefits from the transparency and fractional ownership capabilities that tokenization enables. Real estate, traditionally accessible only to wealthy investors or through public REITs, can be divided into smaller units that provide direct ownership exposure without the volatility of public markets. Even traditional securities—bonds, in particular—are being tokenized by major financial institutions seeking to demonstrate the technology's viability in regulated contexts.

Custody solutions represent one of the critical infrastructure pieces that previously limited institutional adoption. Early tokenization efforts required asset owners to choose between regulated custodians unfamiliar with blockchain technology and crypto-native firms lacking the regulatory credentials that institutional mandates required. The emergence of hybrid solutions—regulated custodians with blockchain capabilities, or crypto firms securing banking licenses—has resolved much of this tension. Investors can now hold tokenized assets with the same level of custodial protection they expect from traditional securities.

Regulatory evolution has been equally important. The European Union's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA), despite its limitations, provides a framework for security token offerings that other jurisdictions are watching closely. Singapore, Switzerland, and several Middle Eastern financial centers have established sandbox programs that allow experimentation within defined boundaries. Even the traditionally cautious Securities and Exchange Commission in the United States has indicated willingness to engage with tokenization, though the path to widespread approval remains complex.

The remaining challenges are significant but increasingly surmountable. Legal frameworks for recognizing blockchain-based ownership records vary across jurisdictions, creating complexity for cross-border transactions. Interoperability between different blockchain networks—essential for a truly liquid market—requires technical standards that are still being developed. And the fundamental question of whether tokenization creates genuine value, or merely shifts existing functions to new technology, deserves continued scrutiny. Nevertheless, the trajectory is clear: tokenization is moving from experimental technology to operational infrastructure, with implications for how assets are issued, traded, and managed across the financial system.
