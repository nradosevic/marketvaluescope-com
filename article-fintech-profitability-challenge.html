The Fintech Profitability Challenge: Can Neobanks Survive?

By Sarah Nakamura March 4, 2026 Fintech

The promise of digital banking seemed irresistible: lower costs, superior user experiences, and the ability to serve customers that traditional banks overlooked. Billions of dollars flowed into neobanks and challenger banks globally, funding customer acquisition campaigns that built impressive user numbers. Yet as the funding environment has tightened, a critical question has emerged—can these institutions actually make money?

The unit economics of digital banking have proven more challenging than early projections suggested. Customer acquisition costs, while lower than traditional banks' branch networks, still run into hundreds of dollars per active account. More problematically, revenue per user has remained stubbornly low. Many neobank customers use accounts for specific purposes—receiving direct deposits, avoiding overdraft fees, or accessing early wage access—without generating the cross-selling revenue that makes traditional bank relationships profitable.

Interest rate increases have provided a temporary tailwind. Banks that struggled to generate net interest margin in the zero-rate environment now earn meaningful spreads on deposits. However, this benefit is partially offset by higher funding costs as competition for deposits intensifies and customers become more rate-sensitive. The promotional rates that attracted initial deposits must eventually normalize, and customer stickiness at that point remains uncertain.

Lending represents both opportunity and risk for digital banks. Credit cards and personal loans generate attractive yields, but also require sophisticated underwriting capabilities and capital reserves. Several neobanks have stumbled on credit, experiencing higher-than-expected default rates as their algorithms proved less predictive than anticipated. Building a sustainable lending business takes years of data accumulation and constant model refinement.

Regulatory compliance adds another layer of cost that scales poorly with digital-first business models. Banks face extensive requirements around anti-money laundering, consumer protection, and capital adequacy that require significant investment in people and systems. Some neobanks have partnered with established banks to manage regulatory complexity, but these partnerships come with their own costs and constraints.

The path to profitability increasingly runs through diversification and premiumization. Successful digital banks are adding subscription tiers with enhanced features, expanding into adjacent services like investment management and insurance, and targeting higher-value customer segments willing to pay for convenience and experience. The mass-market, free-everything model is giving way to more nuanced approaches that balance growth with sustainability.

Consolidation appears inevitable. The market cannot support dozens of subscale digital banks competing for the same customer segments. Larger players with established revenue streams will likely acquire smaller competitors for their technology, customer bases, or specific capabilities. Traditional banks, having initially dismissed digital challengers, are now among the potential acquirers, seeking to accelerate their own digital transformations. The neobank survivors will be those who solve the profitability puzzle before capital runs out.
