The Shift Toward Embedded Finance in Retail

By Priya Sharma March 5, 2026 Technology

Walk into any major retailer today and you'll encounter financial services at nearly every turn. The checkout screen offers installment payments. The app pushes a store-branded credit card with instant approval. The loyalty program doubles as a savings account earning interest on rewards balances. This isn't coincidence—it's the manifestation of embedded finance, a trend reshaping the boundary between commerce and banking.

Embedded finance refers to the integration of financial services into non-financial platforms and customer experiences. Rather than redirecting customers to banks or fintech apps, companies embed lending, payments, insurance, and even investment products directly into their own ecosystems. For retailers, this transformation represents both a new revenue stream and a powerful tool for deepening customer relationships.

The numbers underscore the magnitude of this shift. Industry analysts project the embedded finance market will exceed $7 trillion in transaction value by 2028, up from roughly $2.5 trillion in 2023. Retailers are capturing an increasing share of this pie, motivated by the attractive economics: financial services typically generate margins far exceeding retail's notoriously thin profits. A furniture retailer offering point-of-sale financing can earn more from the loan than from the furniture itself.

Buy-now-pay-later services pioneered this trend at checkout, but the scope has expanded dramatically. Major retailers now offer comprehensive banking services through partnerships with licensed institutions. Walmart's partnership with Ribbit Capital to create a fintech startup, Amazon's lending programs for merchants, and Shopify's integrated business banking exemplify how commerce giants are building financial infrastructure that rivals traditional banks.

For consumers, embedded finance offers undeniable convenience. Applying for credit within an app they already use, receiving instant decisions, and managing everything from a single interface eliminates the friction that traditional financial services impose. Younger consumers particularly gravitate toward these integrated experiences, having grown up expecting seamless digital interactions. The days of visiting a bank branch or even a bank's website increasingly feel antiquated.

However, this convergence raises important questions about consumer protection and systemic risk. When retailers become de facto financial institutions, regulatory oversight becomes complex. Customers may not fully understand the terms of financial products presented in shopping contexts. The data advantages that retailers enjoy—knowing your purchase history, browsing behavior, and financial capacity—create potential for pricing discrimination and privacy concerns that regulators are only beginning to address.

Traditional banks face strategic decisions as embedded finance accelerates. Some have partnered with retailers to provide the banking licenses and balance sheet capacity that power embedded offerings. Others view the trend as an existential threat and are investing heavily in their own digital experiences. The coming years will reveal whether banks successfully defend their customer relationships or cede significant ground to retailers who understand commerce-first financial services in ways that traditional institutions struggle to match.
