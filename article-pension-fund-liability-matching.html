Pension Funds Face a Liability Matching Reckoning

By Margaret O'Sullivan March 5, 2026 Investing

The dramatic rise in interest rates over the past several years has transformed the pension fund landscape in ways that many industry observers are only beginning to appreciate. While higher rates have improved headline funding ratios for most defined benefit plans, they have also exposed fundamental mismatches between asset portfolios and liability profiles that require urgent attention from plan sponsors and fiduciaries.

During the extended period of near-zero interest rates, pension funds faced an existential challenge: their liabilities, calculated using discount rates tied to high-quality bond yields, grew faster than their assets could reasonably be expected to return. Many plans responded by increasing allocations to riskier assets—equities, private equity, real estate, and hedge funds—in search of the returns needed to close funding gaps. This "reach for yield" made mathematical sense in the moment but created significant duration mismatches between assets and liabilities.

The rate increases that began in 2022 and continued through recent years have sharply reduced the present value of pension liabilities, dramatically improving funding ratios at many plans. Corporate pension funds in aggregate moved from roughly 85 percent funded to over 100 percent funded, representing hundreds of billions of dollars in improved financial position. However, this improvement often came despite, not because of, investment returns, as rising rates hammered bond portfolios even while reducing liability values.

Forward-thinking pension managers are now grappling with a strategic inflection point. Having achieved full funding for the first time in many years, should plans lock in this status through liability-driven investing (LDI) strategies that match asset durations closely to liability profiles? Or should they maintain higher-risk allocations in hopes of building surplus that could support benefit improvements or contribution holidays?

The UK's pension crisis in late 2022, when rapidly rising rates created margin calls on LDI derivatives that forced fire sales of gilts, has made some pension trustees wary of aggressive liability matching. However, the lesson most experts draw from that episode is not to avoid LDI but to implement it more carefully, with adequate liquidity buffers and leverage constraints that can withstand rapid rate movements.

For corporate plan sponsors, the improved funding environment presents an opportunity to reduce pension risk that may not recur. Several major corporations have announced plans to transfer pension obligations to insurance companies through pension risk transfer transactions, effectively removing the pension from their balance sheets entirely. While such transactions require paying a premium over calculated liabilities, the certainty they provide has proven attractive to many CFOs tired of pension volatility affecting quarterly earnings.

The coming years will likely see significant restructuring of pension fund portfolios as fiduciaries adapt to the new rate environment. Those that use this window to implement more robust liability matching strategies may find themselves better positioned for whatever interest rate environment emerges next. Those that maintain aggressive positioning in hopes of further gains may be taking risks that their beneficiaries cannot afford.
