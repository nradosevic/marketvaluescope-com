The Small-Cap Value Renaissance: Why Neglected Stocks Are Surging

By Richard Kowalski February 25, 2026 Investing

For the better part of a decade, small-cap value stocks represented the most unloved corner of the equity market. While mega-cap growth stocks—the familiar names dominating technology, e-commerce, and social media—delivered spectacular returns, small-cap value languished. Investors who maintained disciplined allocations to the factor watched their portfolios underperform benchmarks year after year, testing convictions that academic research and historical evidence had long supported. Now, however, the rotation appears to have arrived, with small-cap value delivering returns that recall the factor's historical dominance.

Understanding why small-cap value underperformed for so long illuminates the current opportunity. The post-financial crisis environment favored large companies with global scale, strong balance sheets, and exposure to technology-driven growth trends. Low interest rates and abundant capital flows supported growth stock valuations, as the present value of future earnings grew relative to near-term cash flows. Passive investing's rise channeled assets toward the largest companies, creating a self-reinforcing dynamic that further disadvantaged smaller names. Small-cap value, concentrated in sectors like financials, industrials, and energy, simply didn't fit the dominant investment narrative.

The conditions that suppressed small-cap value have reversed in material ways. Rising interest rates have compressed the valuations of long-duration growth stocks while favoring companies with nearer-term earnings. Bank profitability has improved as net interest margins expanded. Industrial companies benefit from reshoring trends and infrastructure investment. Energy companies, long out of favor due to ESG concerns and capital discipline, have generated substantial free cash flow that supports both distributions and reinvestment. The economic backdrop has shifted to favor precisely the sectors where small-cap value is concentrated.

Valuation starting points matter enormously for subsequent returns, and small-cap value entered this cycle at historically attractive levels. Price-to-book ratios, price-to-earnings multiples, and other valuation metrics had reached extremes that historically preceded strong relative performance. The spread between small-cap value and large-cap growth valuations widened to levels that provided substantial margin of safety even before fundamental improvements materialized. Investors willing to tolerate years of underperformance while maintaining discipline are now seeing the payoff.

The mechanics of rotation create momentum that can persist. As small-cap value outperforms, portfolio managers who had reduced allocations face pressure to rebalance or face tracking error against benchmarks. Quantitative strategies that incorporate momentum signals increasingly favor the factor. Media coverage of outperformance attracts retail attention. These dynamics create buying pressure that can sustain relative performance beyond what pure fundamentals might justify, at least for a period.

Active management advantages may be particularly pronounced in small-cap value. The segment remains under-researched relative to large-cap stocks, with many companies receiving minimal analyst coverage. Inefficiencies created by neglect provide opportunities for managers willing to conduct fundamental research that passive strategies cannot replicate. Index construction challenges in small-cap value—including the inclusion of distressed companies facing genuine business impairment—create opportunities for active strategies that can distinguish between temporarily undervalued businesses and permanent value traps.

Whether the small-cap value revival represents a cyclical bounce or the beginning of a sustained period of outperformance remains contested among market strategists. Bears point to structural headwinds: the continuing importance of scale in many industries, ongoing technological disruption of traditional businesses, and demographic trends that may favor different corporate profiles. Bulls counter that extended periods of underperformance historically preceded extended periods of outperformance, and that current valuations price in pessimistic scenarios that may not materialize. For investors, the debate underscores the importance of maintaining diversified exposure rather than abandoning factor allocations during inevitable periods of disappointing performance.
